Semana Santa

Week of religious acts, concerts, liturgies, a walk of thrones and images through the streets of Ronda. Sponsored by the Brotherhoods and Brotherhoods of Ronda.

Like all Andalucian towns, Ronda celebrates Holy Week with processions, most of which begin in the late afternoon and take hours to wend through the streets and alleys of the old city. Due to its historic charm Ronda is an excelent place to watch the proscssions. Many cross the Tajo at puente nuevo into Plaza Espana. The Baclcony of the rooms of the Parador are an excxcelent place to watch these processions. The Legionairs procession on the night of Jueves Santo and the Widows on Viernes Santo are highlights of Ronda Semana Santa.

May

Royal May Fair

They take place in the second half of May - when the Christian conquest is commemorated - and have been held since the time of the Catholic Monarchs, with horse dressage and bullfighting contests. Located in the fairgrounds, it is the oldest fair in Andalusia in the livestock, agri-food, artisanal and tourist fields, where we can buy items from the mountains, see news about mechanical traction, taste local products, wines, sausages, cheeses etc ... admire livestock , participate in horse shows, logging etc ... see the new business and tourism proposals. Developed by the Party Delegation of the Hon. Ronda City Council.

Ronda Romántica

Ronda is known to be one of the oldest towns in Spain, with remains from prehistoric settlements of the Neolithic period evident in the cave paintings of the nearby 'Cueva de la Pileta'. The Celts were the first settlers of Ronda in the 6th Century B.C., giving the town its original name of Arunda. The now-ruins of the nearby Acinipo (land of the wines) was later founded by the Phoenicians. In the Second Century B.C. the Romans came to Ronda and were engaged in bloody wars with the Carthaginians. After the defeat of the Carthaginians in the Second Punic War, the Romanisation of Acinipo in particular led to a period in which it was developed into a major municipality and had such importance that coins could be minted there. Ronda itself had been a fortified post for the Romans during the Second Punic War and it too developed as a municipality. Periods of peace, unrest and invasion followed for both towns with the eventual decline of the Roman Empire leading to the pillaging and destruction of both towns. Acinipo was eventually abandoned by the 6th century while Ronda however would be entering a period of great prosperity with the arrival of the Moors.

September

Pedro Romero Fair

Dedicated to the mythical bullfighter from Ronda Pedro Romero, who is credited with introducing the crutch in the bullfighting festival, it is the most prominent and has achieved great fame, especially for the performance of the Goyesque Bullfights, when the matadors and all their The gang dress in period attire collected from Goya's paintings. Even part of the public that attends the bullfights dresses according to that magical return to the past. Declared of national tourist interest of Andalusia.

October

It continues to be celebrated that the origins of the fairs and festivals in Ronda date back to the Catholic Monarchs and that they began in this emblematic neighborhood of Ronda.
